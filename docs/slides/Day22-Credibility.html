Boundary Work

  • Thomas Gieryn, Cultural Cartographies of Science
  • Acknowledges the lack of stable criteria for demarcating science from non-science.
  • Different people justify what makes something scientific vs unscientific differently.
  • As credibility contests emerge, people make claims based on their own maps of science.
  • Culturally chart the boundaries around what we consider scientific.

class: center, middle # Science is a symptom of the legitimate power to decide reality - its edges and contents disputed, moved all over the place, settled here and there as decisions about truthful and reliable claims are acted upon … (Gieryn, Cultural Boundaries of Science)

class: center, middle # How do we draw the boundaries of credibility in data work? Who gets seen as a legitimate claims-maker in data science work, and whose voices are often denied a seat at the table?

Deficit Model

  • Belief that the reason people distrust science is because they lack knowledge
  • Often casts scientific illiteracy as both a technical problem and a moral problem
  • Presumed solution to increasing public trust in science is to teach more science

STS rejects the deficit model…

  • Ignores public’s situated knowledge
  • Suggests knowledge is context-free
  • Doesn’t get at the root of distrust

class: center, middle # How do we decide who is considered to have expertise?

Enactments of Expertise

.pull-left[

  • Expertise as a signal of authority
  • Enacted through credentialing, use of certain vocabularies, creation of exclusive social circles, clothing, norms of behavior
  • Enactments also serve a gate-keeping role in determining who gets to contribute to science/data science ]

Citizen Science

  • Exclusionary science as a democratic problem
  • Examples of citizen engagement:
    • Counting declining bird populations
    • Air and water quality monitoring
    • Writing health books for underrepresented populations
  • Must learn how to enact expertise to earn a seat at the table

Social Movement Theory

  • Sociological research that studies how social movements form, operate, sustain, and fizzle out
  • Political Process Theory: suggests that certain political formations enable social movements to form and operate
  • Resource Mobilization Theory: suggests that access to certain resources enable social movements to mobilize
  • Framing: Suggests that collective ideologies and values bring social movements into fruition
